### Biography (composited from public sources)

Tina Salguero grew up on Long Island, New York; her father worked at Columbia University's computer laboratories in Manhattan, and she attended science-focused summer programs at SUNY–Stony Brook before enrolling at Columbia University as an undergraduate. She graduated **summa cum laude** from Columbia in 1997 with a B.A. in Chemistry, then moved to the California Institute of Technology (Caltech), where she earned her Ph.D. in Chemistry in 2003 working in the laboratory of Nobel laureate **Prof. Robert H. Grubbs** (2005 Nobel Prize in Chemistry), studying ruthenium olefin-metathesis catalysts. She then completed a postdoctoral fellowship at the University of California, Santa Barbara, supported by a **Damon Runyon Cancer Research Foundation Fellowship**. Prior to her academic career, she spent several years as a researcher at **HRL Laboratories, LLC** (Malibu, California — a research center historically affiliated with General Motors and Boeing), where she worked on energy technologies for commercial and military applications, including metal-hydride hydrogen storage, microbial fuel cells, and proton-exchange-membrane (PEM) fuel cell materials. She joined the UGA Department of Chemistry as a tenure-track Assistant Professor in **Fall 2010**, becoming one of two new inorganic chemistry hires that year (alongside Prof. Shanta Dhar). She has since been promoted to Associate Professor and holds the additional roles of Associate Department Head, Undergraduate Coordinator, and Academic Director of the Georgia Electron Microscopy (GEM) core facility.

Sources: [UGA Chemistry news, "Two new professors join Department of Chemistry," June 6, 2011](https://www.chem.uga.edu/news/stories/2011/two-new-professors-join-department-chemistry); [UGA Chemistry Faculty Mentor Biographies, Oct. 2021](https://chem.franklin.uga.edu/sites/default/files/Chemistry%20Faculty%20Biographies%20for%20Mentoring%20Program%20(1).pdf); [UGA Chemistry directory](https://www.chem.uga.edu/directory/people/tina-salguero).

### Research Interests (as stated on the official UGA profile)
Research in the Salguero group focuses on hybrid materials that incorporate nanosheet components — well-defined nanomaterials that are one to several monolayers thick and tens of micrometers in lateral dimension. Cited example nanosheet systems include graphene, graphite oxide, metal chalcogenides (MoS₂, NbSe₂), transition-metal oxides (NbWO₆, H₂SrTa₂O₇, Ca₂Nb₃O₁₀), hexagonal boron nitride, and lamellar perovskites. ([UGA Chemistry directory](https://www.chem.uga.edu/directory/people/tina-salguero))

## SECTION 2 — Research Overview

### Major Research Themes
1. **Layered inorganic nanosheets** — chemical synthesis and exfoliation of 2D materials (metal chalcogenides, metal oxides, metal silicates, metal fluorides/oxyfluorides) as building blocks for hybrid functional materials.
2. **Quasi-one-dimensional (1D) van der Waals materials** — a research area Salguero's group has helped define, encompassing transition-metal tri-chalcogenide nanoribbons/nanowires (TaSe₃, NbS₃, ZrTe₃, MoI₃) and related compounds such as the Weyl semimetal (TaSe₄)₂I.
3. **Charge-density-wave (CDW) quantum materials and devices** — synthesis of 1T-TaS₂, 1T-TaSe₂, and related CDW compounds, and collaboration on their integration into electronic switches, oscillators, and radiation-hard devices.
4. **Dielectric nanosheets for energy-dense electronics** — ceramic nanosheet materials intended to enable ultrathin, high-energy-density capacitors and related components (DOE-funded Early Career project).
5. **Electron microscopy methodology** — development of low-voltage/low-kV SEM, STEM, and EELS methods for imaging beam-sensitive nanomaterials and biological specimens, centered on the GEM core facility.
6. **Materials science of cultural heritage objects** — chemical and nanostructural analysis of ancient pigments (e.g., Egyptian Blue, Han Blue) and rock-art binders/pigments.
7. **(Earlier, pre-UGA) Energy materials for hydrogen storage and fuel cells** — metal hydride hydrogen-storage systems and PEM fuel cell component materials, conducted while at HRL Laboratories.

### Scientific Questions Addressed
- How can bulk layered inorganic solids be reliably synthesized, phase-controlled, and exfoliated down to nanosheet or nanoribbon dimensions while retaining useful electronic, dielectric, or optical properties?
- How do reduced dimensionality (2D nanosheets, 1D nanoribbons) and structural anisotropy influence electronic transport, phonon behavior, and charge-density-wave phase transitions?
- Can nanosheet- or nanoribbon-based composites and inks be engineered into functional macroscale devices (electromagnetic shields, printed CDW circuit elements, capacitors)?
- What synthetic-chemistry and microscopy methods are needed to characterize beam-sensitive, air-sensitive, or otherwise "difficult" nanomaterials?
- What can the chemistry of ancient/archaeological pigments reveal about historical technology and materials degradation?

### Materials Studied
Tantalum diselenide/disulfide (TaSe₂, TaS₂, including the 1T and 2H polytypes), tantalum triselenide (TaSe₃), niobium trisulfide (NbS₃, including multiple polymorphs), niobium diselenide (NbSe₂), zirconium tritelluride (ZrTe₃), molybdenum triiodide (MoI₃), (TaSe₄)₂I (a quasi-1D Weyl semimetal), graphene/graphene oxide, hexagonal boron nitride, metal silicate pigments (Egyptian Blue/CaCuSi₄O₁₀, Han Blue/BaCuSi₄O₁₀), metal fluoride/oxyfluoride nanosheets, bismuth tungstate (Bi₂WO₆), manganese phosphate, strontium hexaboride, dielectric ceramic nanosheets, and (earlier) complex metal hydrides and Pt/carbon fuel-cell electrocatalysts.

### Experimental / Characterization Techniques
Chemical vapor transport (CVT) crystal growth; mechanical and liquid-phase (chemical) exfoliation; ink formulation and solution processing of 2D/1D fillers; low-voltage scanning and scanning-transmission electron microscopy (SEM/STEM); electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS); electron diffraction; in-situ heating transmission electron microscopy; Raman and phonon spectroscopy; X-ray diffraction; electrical transport and low-frequency noise measurements (largely performed by collaborating device-physics groups); machine-learning-assisted materials discovery (recent work).

### Instrumentation
The centerpiece instrument associated with Salguero's NSF-funded work is the **Hitachi SU9000EA** ultrahigh-resolution, low-voltage (≤30 kV) field-emission SEM/STEM, equipped with energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS), EELS, electron diffraction, and specialized in-situ heating and tomography sample holders. It is housed at the **Georgia Electron Microscopy (GEM)** core facility, of which Salguero is Academic Director. ([NSF Award #1919942](https://www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/showAward?AWD_ID=1919942); [UGA Today, 2020](https://news.uga.edu/1-million-nsf-grant-funds-new-electron-microscope/))

### Applications
Energy storage (batteries, high-energy-density capacitors); barrier films for flexible electronics, OLEDs, and organic photovoltaics; electromagnetic-interference (EMI) shielding films and composites; radiation-hard ("proton-irradiation-immune") electronic switches; room-temperature CDW oscillators and neuromorphic/logic circuit elements; catalysis supports; biomedical imaging/drug-delivery-adjacent nanomaterials (via GEM collaborations); quantum-network device materials; and (earlier) hydrogen-storage materials and fuel-cell components.

### Research Philosophy / Future Directions (as publicly stated)
In the 2022 review "One-Dimensional van der Waals Quantum Materials" (*Materials Today*), Salguero and coauthors frame quasi-1D van der Waals materials as an emerging materials class analogous to the 2D materials boom of the previous decade, arguing for systematic synthetic-chemistry exploration of this space. A related 2023 paper, "Machine-Learning-Driven Expansion of the 1D van der Waals Materials Space" (*J. Phys. Chem. C*), signals a stated future direction toward computationally guided discovery of new 1D van der Waals compounds. Separately, her 2021 *ACS Nano* commentary on supporting women in STEM indicates an explicitly stated interest in academic mentorship and career-support policy post-pandemic. ([DOI: 10.1016/j.mattod.2022.03.015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mattod.2022.03.015); [DOI: 10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c03882](https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jpcc.3c03882); [DOI: 10.1021/acsnano.1c09686](https://doi.org/10.1021/acsnano.1c09686))

## SECTION 15 — Website Content Recommendations

Based on the totality of publicly available material gathered above, the following structure is recommended for the official Salguero Research Group website:

**Homepage:** A short mission statement centered on the group's identity as pioneers of quasi-1D van der Waals materials and nanosheet chemistry, paired with 2–3 rotating highlight images (microscopy imagery from GEM, or a schematic of a CDW device) and a one-line role summary ("Associate Professor of Chemistry | Associate Department Head | Academic Director, Georgia Electron Microscopy").

**Research page:** Organize by the seven major themes identified in Section 2 (layered nanosheets; quasi-1D van der Waals materials; CDW quantum devices; dielectric/energy materials; electron-microscopy methods; cultural-heritage materials science; legacy hydrogen-storage/fuel-cell work), each with a short paragraph and 1–2 representative figures from open-access papers (rights permitting) or original schematics.

**Projects page:** Use the nine-project structure in Section 3 as a direct template, converting each row into a project card with funding badge (NSF/DOE logo where appropriate) and status indicator (active/completed).

**Publications page:** Implement a searchable/sortable table using the Section 5.4 data as a seed dataset, but the team should re-verify all citation counts and DOIs against Crossref/Google Scholar at build time, since citation counts change continuously and the Google Scholar total could not be independently confirmed here.

**People page:** Populate with the Section 6 current-group table; add photos, one-line bios, and (with individual consent) LinkedIn/ORCID links not currently public. Add an Alumni sub-section once real names/outcomes are confirmed directly with the group (Section 7 flags this as the weakest-sourced part of this dossier).

**Gallery:** Use GEM microscopy imagery (with permission) and the NSF award-report slides identified in Section 13, replacing placeholder/generic-arches stock imagery currently used on the UGA directory page.

**News page:** Seed with the five items in Section 8; establish a process for the group to log its own news going forward, since no dedicated lab-news archive currently exists publicly.

**Funding page:** Use the Section 4 table as a template, with agency logos (NSF/DOE) and plain-language project summaries; consider adding a funding acknowledgment boilerplate for use in publications.

**Teaching page:** Highlight the FYO "UGA at High Magnification" seminar (2024 teaching award) as a flagship public-facing teaching activity, alongside standard course listings.

**Contact page:** Use the office/lab/phone/email details in Section 1; add a physical map pin for iSTEM-2 given the building is relatively new and may not be well indexed in mapping services.
